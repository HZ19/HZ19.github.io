About Me

I am a quantitative social scientist by training. I enjoy working at the intersection of social science, data science, and research methods.

Hello! I am a PhD candidate in Social and Political Science at the European University Institute (EUI), Florence. I am a quantitative social researcher by training and a social data scientist by interest.

My main research interests are computational social science and applied research methods. I am particularly interested in collaborative research that combines methodological innovation with empirical relevance. At the moment, my methods projects include developing a network approach to analyzing deliberative processes, an inequality constrained least squares (ICLS) method for estimating party unity, and a Bayesian nested hierarchical recipe for scaling party policy parameters from manifesto corpus, as well as applying ensemble estimation methods for model aggregation and vacancy chain models for modelling state-bureaucratic systems. I am also working on empirical projects with both observational and experimental designs.

A major interest of mine is accumulation and aggregation in the social sciences. I am intrigued by how knowledge can "accumulate" in social research, and how social scientists can best "aggregate" findings from different studies to improve our collective understanding of some common phenomenon. Right now I am spending part of this interest on the ensemble methods project with crowdsourced socio-epidemiological models, and hope to work more on these issues in the near future.

I am currently a visiting researcher at the Institutions and Political Inequality (IPI) research unit, Berlin Social Science Center (WZB). I am also a predoctoral affiliate at the Nuffield Centre for Experimental Social Science (CESS), Oxford. I hold an MPhil in Comparative Government from Oxford University and a BSc in Politics and Quantitative Research Methods (QRM) from Bristol Univerity.

Research

My latest publications and select works in progress can be found below:

If not now, when? Climate disaster and the Green vote following the 2021 Germany floods

Research & Politics, Dec 13 2022 (Open Access)
With Susanna Garside

In this pre-registered data report, Susanna Garside and I used a DID design to study the short-term electoral effects of the devastating 2021 Germany floods on voter support for Germany’s major environmentalist party, the Green Party. We leveraged the rare occurrence of a large-scale disaster just two months before a federal election and found a marginal increase (only 0.4–1.6 percentage points) in support for the Greens.

When Parties Move to the Middle: The Role of Uncertainty

British Journal of Political Science, Forthcoming
With Johannes Lindvall and David Rueda

In this paper, Johannes Lindvall, David Rueda, and I developed an empirical analysis of how the main parties on the left and the right changed their platforms depending on the information environment they are in, using the outcome of the most recent election to learn about the median voter's ideological position. We also developed a new method that can be used to assess how the uncertainty around the estimated position of the median voter depends on the number of parties in the party system, the ideological positions of the parties, and the proportion of the vote that goes to each party.

Political deliberation as network: an applied methodological note

Working Paper

In this paper, I developed a network-ERGM approach to analyzing data describing deliberative processes. Using two empirical datasets on online and offline deliberative settings, I showed that network models can better account for the endogeneous dynamics in such processes arising from participants' interactions, generate less biased parameter estimates for the key covariates of interest, and yield superior model fit with the data, compared with conventional regression models.

Gathering, evaluating, and aggregating social scientific models of COVID-19 mortality

Working Paper
With Miriam Golden, Alexandra Scacco, Tara Slough, Macartan Humphreys, Eva Vivalt, Kim Yi Dionne, Sampada KC, and Eugenia Nazrullaeva

In this paper, we field tested multiple methods for aggregating competing accounts of a common phenomenon by gathering, evaluating, and filtering explanations for variation in COVID-19 mortality across and within countries. Our results provided evidence for the utility of collecting multiple models and then aggregating them, and showed that aggregation may best not be left to human judgement or to ad hoc processes.
